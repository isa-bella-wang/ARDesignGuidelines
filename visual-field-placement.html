CHAPTER 2

Visual Field Placement

The human field of view is not uniform. Visual acuity, attention capacity, and comfort vary dramatically across different regions of the visual field. Understanding where users can comfortably see, read, and interact with content is essential for effective AR interface design.

This chapter defines the spatial boundaries within which AR content can be placed based on eye rotation limits, head movement constraints, and perceptual comfort zones.

Concepts

Structure of the Visual Field

The human field of vision consists of different areas which are used to see varying degrees of detail and accuracy about the surrounding environment. Both central and peripheral vision are processed simultaneously, each serving distinct but complementary roles in how we perceive and interact with the world.

Central Vision

Central vision is where objects are clearly and sharply seen and used to perform most daily activities. This area uses the fovea—the area of highest acuity. The central vision covers only a very small area of the eye, but it takes up half of the processing in the brain's visual cortex.

Peripheral Vision

The remaining field of view is the peripheral vision. Characterized by a high density of rod cells, it is especially sensitive to motion and changes in luminance, but poor at resolving detail or complex shapes. It guides where central vision should focus next.

Foundation

Why Visual Field Matters in AR

Placing information within AR should be determined by working with these perceptual strengths and limitations rather than against them. When elements are placed in areas the visual system isn't suited for, users must strain to look at them, leading to discomfort or slower performance.

The ±15° Comfort Threshold

Empirical evidence from human-factors standards and visual ergonomics research consistently identifies ±15° of eye rotation as the boundary within which gaze can be comfortably sustained without increased oculomotor strain. These ergonomic standards treat ±15° not as a retinal acuity limit, but as a physiological comfort threshold grounded in the biomechanics of the extra-ocular muscles.

Gaze-behavior research notes that the eyes are typically used only for small-angle gaze shifts, while larger shifts—generally beyond 15–20°—naturally recruit head movement to maintain comfort.

Recommendations

Primary Information Placement

1

Central 30° Zone

The most important and frequently referenced information should appear within the central 30° of the field of vision—between –15° to +15° of the natural line of sight. This corresponds to the optimal eye rotations.

Research shows that positioning the primary UI here reduces task time and errors, minimizes unnecessary head movement, and supports comfortable eye fixation for longer tasks.

IDEAL FOR:

  • Key instructions and guidance
  • Primary text and reading content
  • Main interaction panels
  • Essential controls and buttons
  • Critical notifications

Secondary Information Placement

2

Peripheral Zone (30° to 100°)

The area beyond the central visual field corresponds to the mid to far peripheral—from 30° to 100°. This region is used to detect larger contrasts, colors, and motion, but resolution is lower. Elements in this region should nudge rather than demand attention.

Peripheral vision is incredibly sensitive to motion and luminance changes, making it ideal for supportive visual cues that guide attention without overwhelming the user.

IDEAL FOR:

  • Directional arrows and wayfinding
  • Subtle alerts and notifications
  • Status indicators
  • Progress bars and timers
  • Ambient environmental cues

Visual Sampling & Attention

People take in visual information in "little bites" through a process called visual sampling. Through a largely unconscious process, peripheral vision calls for central vision, deciding what it focuses on next. Even though the peripheral area is not meant for critical information, it remains vitally important as it dictates where central vision will look and what information receives conscious attention.

Design Principle for AR Visual Field Placement

Work with the visual system's natural strengths: place critical information within the central 30° where users can comfortably read and interact, and use the peripheral zones for motion-based cues and ambient awareness. This approach minimizes strain, reduces cognitive load, and creates more natural, comfortable AR experiences that complement rather than fight human physiology.

Key Recommendations

Place primary content and readable text within the central ±15° zone

Use near-peripheral zones (±15° to ±30°) for secondary UI and notifications

Reserve far-peripheral zones (>30°) for ambient awareness and motion detection only

Prefer downward placement over upward for extended viewing comfort

Limit horizontal content spread to ±30° to avoid requiring head movement

Design Principle for AR Depth Placement

This chapter defines the horizontal and vertical bounds of AR design. These spatial constraints determine where text can be legible, where interactions can be comfortable, and how information should be distributed across the visual field. All subsequent guidelines for typography, information density, and interaction placement depend on respecting these field-of-view limits
