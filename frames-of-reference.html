CHAPTER 3

Frames of Reference

Understanding spatial anchoring systems in augmented reality and their impact on user experience, cognitive load, and interaction design.

Concepts

Spatial Frames of Reference

In AR, digital information can be anchored to different spatial reference frames. These frames of reference vary in stability, alignment, and cognitive demands, and each influences how easily the user can understand, track, or interact with AR overlays. The choice of frame directly impacts usability, comfort, and the user's ability to integrate digital content with the physical world.

Head-Locked

Content remains fixed relative to the user's viewpoint, following every head movement. Use sparingly for brief, high-priority notifications only.

Body-Anchored

Content moves with the user's body, providing personally accessible information without environmental dependency. Ideal for menus and personal status.

World-Locked

Content remains fixed in 3D space, aligned with the physical environment. Most preferred for persistent, spatially meaningful information.

Impact

Why Frames of Reference Matter in AR

AR overlays coexist with the real environment, meaning that stability, predictability, and perceptual alignment directly affect usability. Choosing the correct frame of reference minimizes cognitive load, reduces unnecessary gaze shifts, maintains real-world awareness, and allows AR to complement rather than compete with the physical environment.

User Preference Study Results

World-Locked

Preferred for maintaining real-world visibility and spatial coherence

Body-Anchored

Accessibility without perceptual instability during mobile tasks

Head-Locked

Greater discomfort and divided attention between overlay and environment

Recommendations

Design Guidelines

1 Always Keep the World in Mind

84% of users preferred prioritizing the real world over digital overlays. They want content available but not blocking awareness.

Real-world visibility should be above everything else on the UI hierarchy. In AR, real-world coupling takes priority—information should be anchored to the world or to the relevant spatial location to maintain coherence with environmental structure.

2 Be Consistent

Once an appropriate frame of reference is chosen, it should be applied consistently throughout the experience. Because AR relies heavily on the user's ability to integrate different layers of information, any inconsistency in where information appears increases cognitive load.

Maintaining a stable anchoring choice within a task or interaction flow helps users predict where information will appear, reduces attentional switching, and preserves the natural integration of digital and physical cues.

Design Principle for AR Frames of Reference

Choosing the correct frame of reference is not just a technical decision—it's fundamental to creating comfortable, usable AR experiences. World-locked content provides stability and spatial meaning. Body-anchored content offers accessibility without instability. Head-locked content should be reserved for critical, brief notifications only. Always prioritize real-world visibility and maintain consistency within interaction flows to reduce cognitive load and support natural integration of digital and physical information.
